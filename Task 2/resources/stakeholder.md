**TL;DR Summary:** To follow the ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010 standard, you must systematically identify who cares about the system (Stakeholders), what they care about (Concerns), the rules for describing the system to address those interests (Viewpoints), and the actual descriptive models or documentation that result (Views). Every concern must be linked to a stakeholder, framed by a viewpoint, and addressed by a view.

Here is your practical guide for aligning your architecture description (AD) process with the IEEE 42010 standard.

---

### Step 1: Identify the Stakeholders
A stakeholder is defined as any role, position, individual, organization, or class thereof that has an interest, right, share, or claim in your entity of interest[cite: 2573]. 

**Actions to take:**
* List all parties that have influence or control over the entity[cite: 2654].
* List all parties that are impacted by the entity[cite: 2654].
* Include standard roles such as end users, operators, acquirers, owners, suppliers, architects, developers, builders, maintainers, regulators, taxpayers, and markets[cite: 2575]. 
* Consider including adversaries and competitors if their actions are fundamental to the architecture[cite: 3111].
* Ensure your architecture description explicitly identifies which stakeholders possess concerns that are fundamental to the architecture[cite: 3110].
* Document the known impacts of the architecture on both current and future stakeholders[cite: 3112].

---

### Step 2: Identify Stakeholder Concerns
A concern is any matter of relevance or importance to a stakeholder[cite: 2526]. This is how stakeholder interests are typically expressed[cite: 2655].

**Actions to take:**
* Document matters of interest or importance to your identified stakeholders[cite: 2657].
* Capture interests related to the entity's environment, life cycle, architecture, requirements, design, implementation, and operation[cite: 2552].
* Identify concerns such as suitability, feasibility, potential risks, added value, resilience, extensibility, usability, interoperability, and environmental impacts[cite: 3128].
* Explicitly associate every single identified concern with the specific stakeholder or stakeholders holding that concern[cite: 3133].
* State any known resource limitations or constraints that prevented your team from addressing certain identified stakeholders or concerns, and include a rationale for these omissions[cite: 3114].

---

### Step 3: Select or Specify Architecture Viewpoints
An architecture viewpoint is a set of conventions for the creation, interpretation, and use of an architecture view to frame one or more concerns[cite: 2506]. Think of it as the template or lens used to look at the system.

**Actions to take:**
* Identify viewpoints that will dictate the specific aspects and concerns to be addressed[cite: 2797].
* Ensure that every single concern identified in Step 2 is framed by at least one architecture viewpoint[cite: 3154].
* Document the conventions for each viewpoint, including the syntax, semantics, and AD elements to be used[cite: 2803].
* Include version identification for each architecture viewpoint you use[cite: 3152].
* Group viewpoints by stakeholder perspectives if they share common roles, experiences, or beliefs[cite: 2687, 2701].

---

### Step 4: Create Architecture Views
An architecture view is a separately identifiable body of information comprising a portion of your architecture description[cite: 2497, 2560]. This is the actual output (e.g., a data model, a flow diagram) generated by applying the viewpoint.

**Actions to take:**
* Produce one or more architecture views for each architecture viewpoint you are using[cite: 3164].
* Ensure each view strictly adheres to the conventions set by its governing architecture viewpoint[cite: 3177].
* Verify that every concern identified in your AD is tangibly addressed by at least one view in accordance with its governing viewpoint[cite: 3175].
* Compose views using one or more view components (e.g., specific diagrams, narratives, or models) to selectively present the required information[cite: 3198].
* Record any known issues, unresolved risks, or deviations from the governing viewpoint directly within the view[cite: 3185, 3186].
